Audit Report

## Title
Treasury Balance Inflation via Direct Ledger Transfer Blocks Large SNS Governance Proposals at Submission - (File: rs/sns/governance/token_valuation/src/lib.rs)

## Summary

`try_get_balance_valuation_factors` reads the live treasury balance from the ledger via `icrc1_balance_of` at proposal submission time. Because the SNS treasury accounts are ordinary ICRC-1 accounts that any token holder can transfer into, an unprivileged attacker can inflate the live balance to push the treasury across a regime boundary, causing subsequent large `TransferSnsTreasuryFunds` or `MintSnsTokens` proposals to be rejected at submission with "Amount is too large." The attacker's tokens are not destroyed — they remain in the treasury — making the attack cheap and repeatable.

## Finding Description

**Live balance read at submission time.**
`try_get_balance_valuation_factors` in `rs/sns/governance/token_valuation/src/lib.rs` calls `icrc1_balance_of` on the ledger to obtain the treasury balance: [1](#0-0) 

The result is stored directly as `valuation_factors.tokens`: [2](#0-1) 

**Regime classification uses that live balance.**
`ProposalsAmountTotalUpperBound::in_tokens` in `rs/sns/governance/proposals_amount_total_limit/src/lib.rs` classifies the treasury into three regimes based on XDR value:

- ≤ 100,000 XDR → `NoLimit` (100% of balance allowed)
- ≤ 1,200,000 XDR → `Fraction(0.25)` (25% of balance allowed)
- > 1,200,000 XDR → `Xdr(300,000)` (fixed 300,000 XDR allowed) [3](#0-2) 

**Treasury accounts are publicly deterministic ordinary ICRC-1 accounts.**
The SNS token treasury is the governance canister principal with subaccount `compute_distribution_subaccount_bytes(governance_canister_id, TREASURY_SUBACCOUNT_NONCE)` where `TREASURY_SUBACCOUNT_NONCE = 0`: [4](#0-3) 

The ICP treasury is the governance canister's default account (no subaccount). Both are standard ICRC-1/ICP ledger accounts with no transfer restrictions.

**Proposal validation path.**
`validate_and_render_transfer_sns_treasury_funds` calls `treasury_valuation_if_proposal_amount_is_small_enough_or_err`, which calls `assess_treasury_balance` → `try_get_balance_valuation_factors`, and rejects the proposal at submission if the amount exceeds the regime limit: [5](#0-4) 

**Execution-time check uses the stored (submission-time) valuation, not a fresh one.**
`transfer_sns_treasury_funds_amount_is_small_enough_at_execution_time_or_err` receives the `Valuation` that was snapshotted at submission time and uses it for the execution-time check: [6](#0-5) 

This means proposals submitted *before* the attack (with a `NoLimit` valuation stored) are unaffected. Only proposals submitted *after* the attack are blocked.

## Impact Explanation

**Concrete DoS scenario:**
Suppose an SNS treasury holds 90,000 SNS tokens each worth 1 XDR (total: 90,000 XDR). This places the treasury in the `NoLimit` regime; a proposal to transfer 80,000 tokens is valid at submission.

An attacker sends 20,000 SNS tokens directly to the treasury account via `icrc1_transfer`. The treasury now holds 110,000 tokens (110,000 XDR), crossing the 100,000 XDR boundary into `Fraction(0.25)`. The new upper bound is `110,000 × 0.25 = 27,500 tokens`. Any subsequent proposal to transfer more than 27,500 tokens is **rejected at submission** with "Amount is too large."

The attacker's tokens remain in the treasury. The SNS can still pass proposals to transfer up to 25% of the inflated balance at a time, but large single-transfer proposals are blocked until the excess tokens are gradually moved out — which itself requires multiple governance cycles.

This matches the allowed impact: **High ($2,000–$10,000) — Application/platform-level DoS with concrete SNS governance harm.** [7](#0-6) 

## Likelihood Explanation

The attack requires only:
1. Holding any amount of the SNS token (or ICP for the ICP treasury).
2. Calling `icrc1_transfer` to the treasury account — a standard, permissionless ledger operation.

No privileged access, key compromise, social engineering, or consensus-level attack is required. The treasury accounts are deterministic and publicly computable from the governance canister principal. The attack is cheap: the attacker's tokens are not destroyed, they go to the treasury. The attack is repeatable: if the SNS passes proposals to move the excess tokens out, the attacker can re-donate to re-trigger the regime shift. [8](#0-7) 

## Recommendation

Snapshot the treasury balance at a trusted point (e.g., after each executed `TransferSnsTreasuryFunds` or `MintSnsTokens` proposal) and use that snapshot for regime classification at proposal submission, rather than reading the live ledger balance. Alternatively, maintain an internal accounting record of the treasury balance within the governance canister, updated only through official governance-executed transfers, and use that internal record for valuation. The live ledger balance can still be used for execution-time checks (to ensure sufficient funds exist), but the regime classification for proposal limits should not be manipulable by unsolicited deposits.

## Proof of Concept

1. Identify an SNS whose treasury XDR value is just below 100,000 XDR (i.e., in the `NoLimit` regime).
2. Compute the SNS token treasury account: `Account { owner: governance_canister_id, subaccount: Some(compute_distribution_subaccount_bytes(governance_canister_id, 0)) }`.
3. Call `icrc1_transfer` on the SNS ledger, sending enough SNS tokens to the treasury account to push the total XDR value above 100,000 XDR.
4. Attempt to submit a `TransferSnsTreasuryFunds` proposal for an amount exceeding 25% of the now-inflated balance.
5. Observe the proposal is rejected at submission with "Amount is too large."

This can be reproduced as a deterministic integration test using `StateMachine` (as in `rs/sns/integration_tests/src/sns_treasury.rs`) by adding a direct `icrc1_transfer` to the treasury account between the price setup and the proposal submission steps in `test_transfer_sns_treasury_funds_proposals_that_are_too_big_get_blocked_at_submission`.

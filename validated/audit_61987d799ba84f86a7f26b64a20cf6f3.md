### Title
Malicious Coordinator Permanently Denies Signing by Consuming Presignatures Without Producing a Signature — (`src/ecdsa/ot_based_ecdsa/sign.rs`, `src/ecdsa/robust_ecdsa/sign.rs`)

---

### Summary

In both the OT-based ECDSA and Robust ECDSA sign protocols, non-coordinator participants irrevocably consume their one-time presignatures by sending signature shares privately to the coordinator, then immediately return `None` with no confirmation that the coordinator produced a valid signature. A malicious coordinator can collect all shares and refuse to output a signature, permanently denying signing for every honest participant whose presignature is now spent and must never be reused.

---

### Finding Description

The sign protocol is split into two asymmetric roles: the coordinator, who aggregates shares and produces the final signature, and all other participants, who compute and send their share then exit.

In `do_sign_participant` (OT-based ECDSA):

```rust
fn do_sign_participant(...) -> Result<SignatureOption, ProtocolError> {
    let s_i = compute_signature_share(participants, me, presignature, msg_hash)?;
    let wait0 = chan.next_waitpoint();
    chan.send_private(wait0, coordinator, &s_i)?;
    Ok(None)   // ← participant exits here, presignature is spent
}
```

The identical pattern appears in the Robust ECDSA variant:

```rust
fn do_sign_participant(...) -> Result<SignatureOption, ProtocolError> {
    let s_me = compute_signature_share(presignature, msg_hash, participants, me)?;
    let wait_round = chan.next_waitpoint();
    chan.send_private(wait_round, coordinator, &s_me)?;
    Ok(None)   // ← participant exits here, presignature is spent
}
```

The presign documentation explicitly states: *"it's crucial that a presignature is never reused."* Once a participant calls `send_private` and returns, the presignature is permanently consumed — there is no rollback, no confirmation receipt, and no mechanism to detect or recover from coordinator non-delivery.

The coordinator-side function `do_sign_coordinator` is the only code path that calls `sig.verify(...)` and returns `Some(sig)`. If the coordinator aborts, withholds the result, or simply crashes after receiving all shares, every honest participant has lost their presignature with nothing to show for it.

This is structurally identical to the reported vulnerability: a privileged party (coordinator / lock manager) can drain a one-time resource (presignature / funds) that honest parties need, with no limit, no warning, and no recovery path.

---

### Impact Explanation

**High — Permanent denial of signing for honest parties.**

Every honest participant who ran the sign protocol has:
1. Consumed their presignature (reuse is a documented security violation).
2. Sent their secret signature share to the coordinator.
3. Received no signature and no proof of coordinator behavior.

To sign again, all participants must re-run the full presign protocol (OT-based: requires fresh Beaver triples; Robust: requires a new multi-round presign). A malicious coordinator can repeat this attack indefinitely across every presign batch, achieving permanent denial of signing at negligible cost to themselves while imposing full presign overhead on honest parties each time.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

The coordinator is an ordinary participant selected by the caller — there is no cryptographic binding between the coordinator identity and any trust anchor. Any participant who is assigned the coordinator role (or who can influence that assignment) can execute this attack. In deployments where the coordinator rotates or is chosen dynamically, the attack surface is broad. The attack requires no cryptographic break, no leaked keys, and no external dependency — only the ability to be assigned the coordinator role and then withhold output.

---

### Recommendation

1. **Coordinator accountability**: After the coordinator produces the signature, broadcast it to all participants so each can independently verify `sig.verify(&public_key, &msg_hash)` before considering the presignature spent. Non-coordinator participants should not return until they have received and verified the final signature.
2. **Presignature invalidation flag**: Track whether a presignature has been submitted to a signing session. If the session does not complete with a verifiable signature within a timeout, allow participants to mark the presignature as aborted and log the coordinator identity for accountability.
3. **Coordinator-free aggregation**: Consider a design where each participant broadcasts their share to all others (not just the coordinator), allowing any participant to aggregate and verify the final signature, eliminating the single point of failure.

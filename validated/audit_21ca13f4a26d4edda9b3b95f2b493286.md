Let me look at the presign protocol more carefully to understand what validations exist for the `e` polynomial shares.

### Title
Unvalidated `e` Polynomial Shares in Presign Enable Permanent Denial of Signing — (`src/ecdsa/robust_ecdsa/presign.rs`)

### Summary

A malicious presigner can send crafted evaluations for their `f_e` polynomial (one that does **not** have a zero constant term) to honest participants during presign. Because the presign protocol commits to and validates `k` and `a` shares but never commits to or verifies `e` shares, the corruption is undetectable. The corrupted `e_i` values propagate into every participant's `PresignOutput.e`, causing the Lagrange interpolation of `e` shares at zero to be non-zero during signing, which makes `sig.verify()` fail unconditionally for that presignature.

---

### Finding Description

**Presign — no commitment or verification for `fe` evaluations**

In `do_presign`, five polynomials are generated. `fe` is required to have a zero constant term: [1](#0-0) 

Each participant privately sends evaluations of all five polynomials to every other participant: [2](#0-1) 

Received shares are accumulated without any check: [3](#0-2) 

The protocol then validates `k` shares via exponent interpolation of `big_r_i` values (lines 194–213) and `a` shares via exponent interpolation of `big_w_i` values (lines 274–311): [4](#0-3) [5](#0-4) 

There is **no analogous commitment or verification for `b`, `d`, or `e` shares**. The final `e` share is stored directly: [6](#0-5) 

**`e` is passed through rerandomization unchanged**

`RerandomizedPresignOutput::rerandomize_presign` rerandomizes `big_r`, `alpha`, and `beta`, but copies `e` verbatim: [7](#0-6) 

**Signing equation breaks when `e(0) ≠ 0`**

`compute_signature_share` uses the corrupted `e` directly: [8](#0-7) 

The coordinator sums Lagrange-weighted shares:

```
s = Σ λᵢ · (msg_hash · αᵢ + Rx · βᵢ + eᵢ)
  = msg_hash · α(0) + Rx · β(0) + e(0)
```

When all `f_e_j` have zero constant term, `e(0) = 0` and the equation holds. If a malicious participant sends evaluations from a polynomial whose constant term is non-zero, `e(0) ≠ 0`, and the resulting `s` is wrong. The coordinator then hits: [9](#0-8) 

returning `ProtocolError::AssertionFailed("signature failed to verify")` — a hard error with no fallback.

---

### Impact Explanation

Every signing attempt using the corrupted presignature fails unconditionally. Because presignatures must not be reused (by design), the corrupted presignature is permanently unusable. The malicious participant can repeat the attack for every presign round, causing indefinite denial of signing. No mechanism in the protocol identifies which participant sent the bad `fe` evaluations (they are sent privately), so honest parties cannot exclude the attacker.

**Impact: High — Permanent denial of signing for honest parties under valid protocol inputs and documented trust assumptions.**

---

### Likelihood Explanation

The attacker only needs to be one of the `2t+1` presigners (the minimum required set). The manipulation requires sending a single modified scalar per victim participant in Round 1 of presign — a trivial deviation from the protocol. The attack is undetectable with the current code.

---

### Recommendation

Add a commitment to the `fe` polynomial's evaluations analogous to the existing `big_r_i` / `big_w_i` exponent-interpolation checks:

1. Each participant broadcasts a commitment to their `fe` polynomial (e.g., `g^{f_e_j(i)}` for each `i`, or a Pedersen commitment to the constant term being zero).
2. After accumulating `e` shares, each participant verifies that the received `f_e_j(i)` values are consistent with a zero-constant-term polynomial using exponent interpolation (the same pattern already used for `k` and `a` at lines 194–213 and 274–311).
3. Alternatively, use a zero-knowledge proof that each participant's `fe` polynomial has a zero constant term, broadcast alongside the Round 1 evaluations.

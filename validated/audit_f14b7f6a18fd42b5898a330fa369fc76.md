### Title
Cross-COA Signature Replay in `validateCOAOwnershipProof` Allows Impersonation of a Victim's Secondary COA - (File: `fvm/evm/stdlib/contract.cdc`)

### Summary

`EVM.validateCOAOwnershipProof` verifies that the provided signatures are valid for the claimed Cadence account's keys and that the COA at the claimed `path` holds the claimed `evmAddress`. However, it does not enforce that `signedData` commits to `evmAddress`, and the `CapabilityPath` field inside the RLP-encoded proof is **not covered by any signature**. An attacker who observes a victim's valid proof for one COA can modify the `CapabilityPath` field and replay the unchanged signatures to pass the ownership check for a **different COA** belonging to the same Cadence account.

### Finding Description

`EVM.validateCOAOwnershipProof` accepts six caller-supplied arguments: `address`, `path`, `signedData`, `keyIndices`, `signatures`, and `evmAddress`. [1](#0-0) 

The function performs two checks:

1. It verifies that the `signatures` are cryptographically valid over `signedData` using keys from `address`.
2. It borrows the public capability at the caller-supplied `path` from `address` and checks that the COA resource there reports `evmAddress`. [2](#0-1) 

The code itself acknowledges the gap:

> "Note: this function does not enforce that `signedData` includes `evmAddress`. In principle, a signature produced for one purpose could be replayed here against a different COA owned by the same Cadence account." [3](#0-2) 

The on-chain proof structure is RLP-encoded and contains `CapabilityPath` as a plain field: [4](#0-3) 

Because `CapabilityPath` is **not signed**, an attacker can decode the RLP proof, swap `CapabilityPath` from `/public/coa1` to `/public/coa2`, re-encode it, and submit it. The signature bytes remain unchanged and still verify correctly against the victim's account keys. The EVM address check then passes because the COA at `/public/coa2` legitimately holds `E2`.

The EVM-side entry point is the `verifyCOAOwnershipProof(address,bytes32,bytes)` precompile, which decodes the proof and forwards all fields—including the attacker-modified `CapabilityPath`—to `validateCOAOwnershipProof`: [5](#0-4) 

The COA's ERC-1271 `isValidSignature` implementation calls this precompile with `address(this)` as the EVM address, so any EVM contract performing ERC-1271 verification against COA2 will receive `true` for the replayed proof: [6](#0-5) 

### Impact Explanation

If a Cadence account controls two COAs (e.g., COA1 at `/public/coa1` with EVM address `E1`, and COA2 at `/public/coa2` with EVM address `E2`), an attacker who observes any on-chain proof the victim submitted for COA1 can replay it—with only the `CapabilityPath` field changed—to satisfy ERC-1271 `isValidSignature` checks on COA2. Any EVM contract that gates asset transfers, governance votes, or privileged operations behind ERC-1271 ownership of COA2 will incorrectly authorize the attacker. This is a direct analog to the Solana bug: the signer (victim's keys) is verified, but the binding between the signer and the specific identity being claimed (COA2) is not enforced.

### Likelihood Explanation

The victim must have published capabilities for at least two COAs under different public paths. The attacker must have observed a valid proof the victim submitted for one of those COAs (all on-chain data is public). No privileged access, leaked keys, or social engineering is required. The attacker only needs to decode the RLP proof, change one field, and re-encode it—a trivial off-chain operation.

### Recommendation

Require that `signedData` commits to `evmAddress` (e.g., `signedData = hash(evmAddress || applicationData)`). This binds each signature irrevocably to a single COA's EVM address, making cross-path replay cryptographically impossible regardless of how many COAs a Cadence account holds.

### Proof of Concept

1. Victim account `A` creates COA1 at `/public/coa1` (EVM address `E1`) and COA2 at `/public/coa2` (EVM address `E2`).
2. Victim submits a transaction that calls `verifyCOAOwnershipProof(E1, D, proof1)` where `proof1 = RLP{Address:A, CapabilityPath:/public/coa1, KeyIndices:[0], Signatures:[sig_D]}`. This proof is now public on-chain.
3. Attacker decodes `proof1`, sets `CapabilityPath = /public/coa2`, re-encodes to produce `proof2`. Signatures are unchanged.
4. Attacker calls `isValidSignature(D, proof2)` on COA2 (EVM address `E2`).
5. COA2's `isValidSignature` calls `verifyCOAOwnershipProof(E2, D, proof2)`.
6. The precompile invokes `validateCOAOwnershipProof(A, /public/coa2, D, [0], [sig_D], E2)`.
7. Step 1 (signature check): `sig_D` is valid over `D` for `A`'s key 0 — **passes**.
8. Step 2 (EVM address check): COA at `/public/coa2` reports `E2` — **passes**.
9. Function returns `isValid: true`. The EVM contract incorrectly concludes the attacker has proven ownership of COA2.

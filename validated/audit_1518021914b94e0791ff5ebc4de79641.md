### Title
Fee-on-Transfer NEP-141 Token Bridging Causes ERC-20 Over-Minting and Bridge Insolvency - (File: `engine/src/engine.rs`)

---

### Summary

The `ft_on_transfer` entry point in Aurora Engine unconditionally trusts the `amount` field supplied by the calling NEP-141 token contract to determine how many ERC-20 (or base) tokens to mint inside the EVM. For a fee-on-transfer NEP-141 token, the actual balance credited to Aurora is `amount - fee`, but the engine mints the full `amount`. This creates a permanent surplus of ERC-20 supply over the NEP-141 reserves held by Aurora, making the bridge insolvent and causing the last withdrawers to have their funds permanently frozen.

---

### Finding Description

The NEP-141 standard's `ft_transfer_call` flow works as follows:

1. The caller invokes `ft_transfer_call(receiver_id=aurora, amount, msg)` on the NEP-141 contract.
2. The NEP-141 contract deducts `amount` from the caller's balance, credits `amount` (or `amount - fee` for fee-on-transfer tokens) to Aurora's balance, then calls `ft_on_transfer(sender_id, amount, msg)` on Aurora.
3. Aurora's `ft_on_transfer` handler reads `args.amount` from the callback and mints that many tokens.

The critical flaw is in step 3. Aurora never queries its own actual NEP-141 balance before and after the transfer to determine the real received amount. It blindly mints `args.amount` tokens regardless of what was actually deposited.

In `engine/src/contract_methods/connector.rs`, `ft_on_transfer` dispatches to either `receive_base_tokens` or `receive_erc20_tokens`: [1](#0-0) 

In `receive_erc20_tokens`, the amount used for minting is taken directly from `args.amount` without any balance verification: [2](#0-1) 

This `amount` is then passed to `setup_receive_erc20_tokens_input` which calls `mint(recipient, amount)` on the ERC-20 contract: [3](#0-2) 

The same pattern applies to `receive_base_tokens`: [4](#0-3) 

On the withdrawal side, `EvmErc20.withdrawToNear` burns `amount` ERC-20 tokens and passes that same `amount` to the `ExitToNear` precompile: [5](#0-4) 

The precompile then calls `ft_transfer(amount)` on the NEP-141 contract: [6](#0-5) 

If Aurora's actual NEP-141 balance is less than the total ERC-20 supply (due to fee-on-transfer inflation), the `ft_transfer` call will fail for the last withdrawers, permanently freezing their funds.

---

### Impact Explanation

**Critical — Insolvency and Permanent Freezing of Funds.**

For every deposit of `N` tokens from a fee-on-transfer NEP-141 (fee rate `f`):
- Aurora receives `N × (1 - f)` NEP-141 tokens.
- Aurora mints `N` ERC-20 tokens.
- The deficit per deposit is `N × f`.

After `k` deposits the total ERC-20 supply exceeds Aurora's NEP-141 reserves by `k × N × f`. The bridge is structurally insolvent: the last `k × N × f` worth of ERC-20 tokens can never be redeemed. Those holders' funds are permanently frozen inside the EVM with no recovery path, because `ft_transfer` on the NEP-141 contract will revert when Aurora's balance is insufficient.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

**Medium.** The NEAR ecosystem supports arbitrary NEP-141 tokens, and fee-on-transfer tokens are a known token design. Any token deployer can register their fee-on-transfer NEP-141 with Aurora via `deploy_erc20_token` — this requires no special privilege. Once registered, any user who bridges that token triggers the accounting discrepancy. The attacker-controlled entry path is fully permissionless: call `ft_transfer_call` on the NEP-141 contract pointing to Aurora as receiver.

---

### Recommendation

In `receive_erc20_tokens` and `receive_base_tokens`, do not trust `args.amount` as the minted quantity. Instead, record the NEP-141 balance of Aurora before the callback is invoked and compare it to the balance after. Use the difference as the actual received amount for minting. Since `ft_on_transfer` is a callback (the transfer has already occurred by the time it is called), the balance difference is observable at callback time.

Concretely, before minting, query the NEP-141 balance of the current Aurora account, compare it to the stored pre-transfer balance (which must be recorded in the `ft_transfer_call` initiation), and use `actual_received = post_balance - pre_balance` as the mint amount. Alternatively, document and enforce that only non-fee-on-transfer NEP-141 tokens may be registered via `deploy_erc20_token`, and add a runtime guard that rejects tokens whose `ft_on_transfer` callback reports an `amount` inconsistent with the balance change.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Deploy a fee-on-transfer NEP-141 token `fee_token.near` that deducts 10% on every transfer.
2. Call `aurora.deploy_erc20_token(fee_token.near)` to register it. This is permissionless.
3. Alice calls `fee_token.near.ft_transfer_call(receiver_id=aurora, amount=1000, msg=alice_evm_address)`.
   - `fee_token.near` transfers 900 tokens to Aurora (10% fee deducted).
   - `fee_token.near` calls `aurora.ft_on_transfer(sender=alice, amount=1000, msg=alice_evm_address)`.
4. Aurora's `ft_on_transfer` → `receive_erc20_tokens` reads `args.amount = 1000` and mints 1000 ERC-20 tokens for Alice.
   - Aurora holds 900 NEP-141 tokens; ERC-20 supply = 1000. Deficit = 100.
5. Alice calls `erc20.withdrawToNear(alice_near_account, 1000)`:
   - Burns 1000 ERC-20 tokens.
   - `ExitToNear` precompile calls `fee_token.near.ft_transfer(receiver_id=alice, amount=1000)`.
   - Aurora only holds 900 tokens → `ft_transfer` reverts.
   - Alice's 1000 ERC-20 tokens are burned but she receives nothing. Funds permanently frozen.

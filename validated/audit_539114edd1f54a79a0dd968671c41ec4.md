### Title
Missing Zero-Address Validation for `reward_address` Allows Permanent Freezing of Unclaimed Yield - (File: `src/staking/staking.cairo`, `src/pool/pool.cairo`)

### Summary
Both `change_reward_address` in the staking contract and `change_reward_address` in the pool contract accept any `ContractAddress` as the new reward address, with no check that the address is non-zero. A staker or pool member who sets their reward address to `0` will have all future reward transfers directed to the zero address, permanently destroying unclaimed yield.

### Finding Description
`change_reward_address` in `src/staking/staking.cairo` validates only that the new address is not a registered token address: [1](#0-0) 

There is no `assert!(reward_address.is_non_zero(), ...)` guard. The same omission exists in the pool contract: [2](#0-1) 

The initial `stake()` entry point also accepts `reward_address` without a zero-address check: [3](#0-2) 

And `enter_delegation_pool` in the pool contract similarly lacks the check: [4](#0-3) 

When rewards are later distributed, `send_rewards_to_staker` unconditionally transfers to whatever `reward_address` is stored: [5](#0-4) 

If the STRK ERC20 implementation permits transfers to address `0` (a common pattern on Starknet where address `0` is not blocked at the token level), those tokens are irrecoverably burned. Even if the ERC20 reverts on a zero-address transfer, the revert propagates through `unstake_action` (which calls `send_rewards_to_staker` internally), temporarily blocking the staker from completing their exit until they call `change_reward_address` again.

### Impact Explanation
**High — Permanent freezing of unclaimed yield.** A staker or pool member who sets `reward_address = 0` (by mistake or by being socially engineered) will have every subsequent reward transfer sent to the zero address. Because the staking contract holds no per-user reward balance after the transfer is issued, those tokens cannot be recovered. The same applies to the pool member's `claim_rewards` path: [6](#0-5) 

### Likelihood Explanation
**Medium.** The call is made directly by the staker or pool member (unprivileged, no role required). Human error when passing a zero address is realistic, especially during scripted migrations or tooling bugs. The spec documents no zero-address precondition for `change_reward_address`: [7](#0-6) 

### Recommendation
Add a non-zero assertion at the top of every function that writes a `reward_address`:

```cairo
assert!(reward_address.is_non_zero(), "Reward address cannot be zero");
```

Apply this to:
- `stake()` in `src/staking/staking.cairo`
- `change_reward_address()` in `src/staking/staking.cairo`
- `enter_delegation_pool()` in `src/pool/pool.cairo`
- `change_reward_address()` in `src/pool/pool.cairo`

### Proof of Concept

1. A staker calls `change_reward_address(reward_address: 0)` on the staking contract. No assertion fires.
2. The staker (or anyone) calls `claim_rewards(staker_address)`.
3. `send_rewards_to_staker` executes `token_dispatcher.checked_transfer(recipient: 0, amount: rewards)`.
4. If the ERC20 allows the transfer, the reward tokens are sent to address `0` and are permanently inaccessible.
5. If the ERC20 reverts, `claim_rewards` and `unstake_action` both revert, temporarily freezing the staker's ability to exit until they correct the address.

The identical path exists for pool members via `pool.change_reward_address(0)` followed by `pool.claim_rewards(pool_member)`. [8](#0-7) [9](#0-8)

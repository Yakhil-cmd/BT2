### Title
Unvalidated `ph` field in blockchain notifications redirects counter-offer notifications to attacker-controlled address — (`packages/gui/src/hooks/useBlockchainNotifications.tsx`)

### Summary
An unprivileged on-chain attacker can craft a blockchain notification with an arbitrary `ph` (puzzleHash) field. The GUI trusts this field unconditionally and uses it as the counter-offer return address. When the victim creates a counter-offer and sends a notification back, the notification (including a small XCH fee and the victim's counter-offer URL) is sent to the attacker's address instead of the legitimate original sender.

### Finding Description
The `prepareNotifications` function in `useBlockchainNotifications.tsx` parses the on-chain notification message and extracts `ph` directly from the attacker-controlled JSON payload without any validation that it matches the actual sender's puzzle hash. This value flows through `NotificationOffer.handleClick` or `OfferIncomingTable.handleCounterOffer` → `CreateOfferBuilder` → `handleOfferCreated` → `OfferShareDialog` → `NotificationSendDialog`, where it is rendered as a non-editable destination address. The victim cannot detect the substitution.

### Impact Explanation
Fits the High scope: "Corruption, spoofing, or unsafe trust of... notification... state that causes a user to... send... the wrong... destination." The victim sends a notification payment to the attacker and the legitimate sender never receives the counter-offer notification. The attacker also receives the victim's counter-offer URL, allowing them to monitor or act on it.

### Likelihood Explanation
Any on-chain actor can send a notification to any puzzle hash for ~0.0001 XCH. No special privileges are required. The victim only needs to have `globalNotifications` enabled (the default) and click Counter or view the offer notification.

### Recommendation
When parsing a blockchain notification, validate that the `ph` field matches the actual on-chain sender's puzzle hash (available from the notification's `id`/coin record). If they do not match, either discard `ph` or disable the counter-offer button. Alternatively, display the `ph` address as an editable field so the victim can correct it before sending.

### Proof of Concept
1. Attacker calls `send_notification` targeting victim's puzzle hash with message `{"v":1,"t":1,"d":{"u":"https://attacker.com/offer","ph":"<attacker_puzzle_hash_hex>"}}`.
2. Victim's GUI receives and displays the notification (globalNotifications enabled).
3. Victim clicks "Counter" in `OfferIncomingTable` or clicks the notification bell.
4. Victim creates a counter-offer and proceeds to the share dialog.
5. `NotificationSendDialog` shows the attacker's address (non-editable) as the recipient.
6. Victim clicks "Send Message" — the notification (XCH fee + counter-offer URL) is sent to the attacker's puzzle hash.
7. Assert: the `target` field in the `sendNotification` RPC call equals the attacker's puzzle hash, not the legitimate sender's.

### Title
Unconstrained CKD Coordinator Can Corrupt Protocol Output Without Participant Verification — (File: `src/confidential_key_derivation/protocol.rs`)

---

### Summary

The CKD protocol designates a single coordinator participant with unchecked power to produce any `CKDOutput` it chooses. Participants send their secret shares privately to the coordinator and immediately return `None` with no subsequent verification round. There is no cryptographic mechanism preventing a malicious coordinator from ignoring contributions, substituting arbitrary group elements, or fabricating the output entirely, causing the client to unmask an incorrect confidential key.

---

### Finding Description

In `do_ckd_participant` (lines 17–33 of `src/confidential_key_derivation/protocol.rs`), each non-coordinator participant computes its Lagrange-weighted share `(norm_big_y, norm_big_c)`, sends it **privately** to the coordinator, and immediately returns `None`:

```rust
fn do_ckd_participant(...) -> Result<CKDOutputOption, ProtocolError> {
    let (norm_big_y, norm_big_c) =
        compute_signature_share(participants, me, key_pair, app_id, app_pk, rng)?;
    let waitpoint = chan.next_waitpoint();
    chan.send_private(waitpoint, coordinator, &(norm_big_y, norm_big_c))?;
    Ok(None)
}
``` [1](#0-0) 

The function is **not async** — there is no second round where participants receive back the coordinator's aggregated result to verify it. Participants have no visibility into what the coordinator does with their contribution.

In `do_ckd_coordinator` (lines 35–58), the coordinator blindly sums whatever it receives from `recv_from_others` with no cryptographic verification of correctness:

```rust
for (_, participant_output) in
    recv_from_others::<CKDOutput>(&chan, waitpoint, &participants, me).await?
{
    norm_big_y += participant_output.big_y();
    norm_big_c += participant_output.big_c();
}
let ckd_output = CKDOutput::new(norm_big_y, norm_big_c);
Ok(Some(ckd_output))
``` [2](#0-1) 

The coordinator is the **sole party** that receives the final `CKDOutput`. No participant can verify that the coordinator correctly included their contribution. The coordinator can:

1. Drop one or more participants' `(norm_big_y, norm_big_c)` shares entirely (using fewer than threshold contributions).
2. Add arbitrary group elements to `norm_big_y` or `norm_big_c`.
3. Produce a completely fabricated `CKDOutput` without running the aggregation at all.

The `ckd` entry point enforces only that the coordinator is a member of the participant list — no cryptographic accountability is imposed: [3](#0-2) 

The `CKDOutput::unmask` function then computes `C − a · Y` on whatever `(Y, C)` the coordinator produced: [4](#0-3) 

If the coordinator fabricated `(Y', C')`, the client derives `C' − a · Y'`, which is an attacker-controlled group element rather than the intended `msk · H(pk, app_id)`.

---

### Impact Explanation

A malicious coordinator can produce any `CKDOutput` it chooses. The client, who unmasks the output with their private app key `a`, will derive an incorrect or attacker-chosen confidential key and have no cryptographic means to detect the manipulation. This directly matches:

> **High: Corruption of CKD outputs so honest parties accept inconsistent or unusable cryptographic outputs.**

The client's derived key is silently wrong. Any downstream use of that key (e.g., decryption, authentication) will fail or produce attacker-controlled results.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

The coordinator role is assigned by the caller at runtime — any participant can be designated coordinator. The coordinator has no cryptographic accountability: it can deviate arbitrarily and the deviation is undetectable by any other party. The threat is realistic whenever the coordinator node is compromised or acts maliciously, which is a documented concern for MPC deployments. Likelihood is **medium**: it requires coordinator compromise, but once compromised, exploitation is trivial and silent.

---

### Recommendation

1. **Commitment before reveal**: Require each participant to broadcast a hash commitment to their `(norm_big_y, norm_big_c)` before sending the values privately to the coordinator. After the coordinator publishes the aggregated output, participants can verify their committed values were included.
2. **Coordinator proof of correct aggregation**: Require the coordinator to broadcast the final `(Y, C)` along with a proof (e.g., a simple re-broadcast of all received contributions) so participants can independently verify the sum.
3. **Multi-coordinator or threshold output**: Require a threshold of participants to independently verify and sign off on the aggregated output before it is accepted, analogous to the multi-sig recommendation in the external report.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Honest participants `P1, P2, P3` each compute `(norm_big_y_i, norm_big_c_i)` and send them privately to the coordinator `C` via `do_ckd_participant`.
2. Malicious coordinator `C` ignores all received shares and calls `CKDOutput::new(arbitrary_Y, arbitrary_C)` with attacker-chosen group elements.
3. The coordinator returns `Some(CKDOutput { big_y: arbitrary_Y, big_c: arbitrary_C })`.
4. The client calls `ckd_output.unmask(app_sk)` and computes `arbitrary_C − app_sk · arbitrary_Y`, obtaining an attacker-controlled value instead of `msk · H(pk, app_id)`.
5. No participant detects the manipulation because `do_ckd_participant` returns `None` immediately after sending, with no verification round.

### Title
Bank Precompile `burn` and `transfer` Accept Arbitrary Address Without Caller Authorization — (`File: x/cronos/keeper/precompiles/bank.go`)

### Summary

The Cronos bank precompile (`0x0000000000000000000000000000000000000064`) exposes `burn(address, uint256)` and `transfer(address, address, uint256)` methods that operate on native `evm/<callerContract>` tokens. Neither method verifies that the token holder has authorized the calling contract to spend their balance. Any EVM contract can burn or transfer native tokens from any user's account without consent.

### Finding Description

`BankContract.Run()` in `x/cronos/keeper/precompiles/bank.go` handles `burn` and `transfer` as follows:

**`burn` path** (lines 113–156):
- Unpacks an arbitrary `recipient` address from calldata as the account to burn from.
- Derives the denom as `evm/<contract.Caller()>`.
- Calls `bankKeeper.SendCoinsFromAccountToModule(ctx, addr, ...)` then `BurnCoins(...)` — draining `addr`'s native balance.
- The only guard is `checkBlockedAddr`, which only rejects module-account addresses. There is **no check** that `addr` equals the transaction originator or that `addr` has granted an allowance to the calling contract.

**`transfer` path** (lines 167–200):
- Unpacks an arbitrary `sender` and `recipient` from calldata.
- Calls `bankKeeper.SendCoins(ctx, from, to, ...)` — moving `evm/<callerContract>` tokens from `from` to `to`.
- Again, **no authorization check** that `from` consented to the transfer.

The denom scoping (`evm/<contract.Caller()>`) means the attack is bounded to tokens of the calling contract's own denom, but within that scope the contract has unconditional power to drain any holder.

Compare with `utils.go`'s `exec` helper (used by other precompiles), which explicitly validates `caller != signer` and rejects mismatches. The bank precompile has no equivalent guard.

### Impact Explanation

**Critical — Unauthorized burn/transfer of precompile-controlled native assets.**

Any EVM contract can:
1. Call `bank.burn(victimAddress, victimBalance)` to destroy a victim's entire `evm/<contract>` native balance with no approval.
2. Call `bank.transfer(victimAddress, attackerAddress, victimBalance)` to redirect a victim's `evm/<contract>` native balance to an attacker-controlled address.

Users who hold `evm/<contract>` tokens in their native bank balance (e.g., after calling `moveToNative` on a contract) have no protection against the contract draining their balance at any time.

### Likelihood Explanation

The entry path is fully unprivileged: any deployed EVM contract can call the bank precompile. A contract that appears legitimate can accumulate user deposits in native `evm/<contract>` form, then drain all holders in a single transaction. No key compromise, governance action, or validator collusion is required.

### Recommendation

Add a caller-authorization check in the `burn` and `transfer` handlers. For `burn`, require that `addr == contract.Caller()` (i.e., a contract may only burn from itself, not from arbitrary users). For `transfer`, require that `sender == contract.Caller()` (i.e., a contract may only transfer out of its own balance). Alternatively, implement an allowance/approval registry in the precompile so token holders can explicitly authorize contracts to spend on their behalf, mirroring the ERC-20 `approve`/`transferFrom` model.

2. Victim calls `moveToNative(100)` → holds `100 evm/0xMalicious` in native bank balance.
3. Attacker calls `MaliciousContract.drainVictim(victim, attacker)`.
4. `bank.transfer(victim, attacker, 100)` executes inside `BankContract.Run()`:
   - `contract.Caller()` = `0xMalicious` → denom = `evm/0xMalicious`
   - `from` = victim, `to` = attacker
   - `bankKeeper.SendCoins(ctx, from, to, ...)` succeeds — no authorization check.
5. Victim's `100 evm/0xMalicious` tokens are transferred to attacker without consent.

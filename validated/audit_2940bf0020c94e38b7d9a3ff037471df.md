### Title
Missing Sequencer Uptime Check in L2 Price Providers Allows Stale-Price Execution After Sequencer Restart — (File: `smart-contracts-poc/contracts/PriceProviderL2.sol`, `smart-contracts-poc/contracts/ProtectedPriceProviderL2.sol`)

---

### Summary

`PriceProviderL2` and `ProtectedPriceProviderL2` are deployed on L2 networks (Arbitrum, Base, etc.) and rely solely on a `MAX_TIME_DELTA` staleness check to guard against stale oracle data. Neither contract checks a Chainlink Sequencer Uptime Feed or enforces a post-restart grace period. When an L2 sequencer goes down and comes back up, the last price stored in the oracle before the outage passes the staleness check if the downtime is shorter than `MAX_TIME_DELTA`, allowing swaps to execute at a price that no longer reflects market reality.

---

### Finding Description

Both L2 price providers read oracle data through `IPricedOracle.price(feedId, pool)` and apply a staleness check:

**`PriceProviderL2._getBidAndAskPrice()`**: [1](#0-0) 

**`ProtectedPriceProviderL2._computeBidAsk()`**: [2](#0-1) 

The staleness check is: [3](#0-2) 

`MAX_TIME_DELTA` is bounded at construction to `[1 second, 7 days]`: [4](#0-3) 

The underlying oracle (`ChainlinkOracle` / `OracleBase`) is a **push-based** store: off-chain reporters push signed reports, and the contract stores the latest price with its `timestampMs`. When the L2 sequencer is down, no new reports can be pushed. When the sequencer restarts, the stored `oracleData[feedId]` still holds the pre-downtime price with its pre-downtime `refTime`. [5](#0-4) 

If the sequencer downtime is shorter than `MAX_TIME_DELTA`, the pre-downtime price passes the staleness check immediately upon restart — before any fresh report can be pushed. There is no sequencer uptime feed check and no grace period enforcement anywhere in either L2 price provider.

The registry ABI confirms a `ChainlinkVerifierL2` with `GRACE_PERIOD` and `sequencerUptimeFeed` exists as a separate contract, but it is not wired into `PriceProviderL2` or `ProtectedPriceProviderL2` — the price providers that pools actually call. [6](#0-5) 

---

### Impact Explanation

When the sequencer restarts after a downtime shorter than `MAX_TIME_DELTA`:

1. The pool's price provider immediately returns the pre-downtime bid/ask (e.g., ETH at $3,000).
2. If the real market price has moved significantly during the outage (e.g., ETH drops to $2,400), an attacker can immediately submit a swap at the stale $3,000 ask price.
3. The pool sells ETH at $3,000 when it is worth $2,400, directly draining LP reserves.
4. LPs suffer a direct loss of principal proportional to the price deviation and the swap size.

This is a **bad-price execution** impact: a stale bid/ask quote reaches a pool swap, causing LP fund loss.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- L2 sequencer outages have occurred on Arbitrum and Base (documented incidents).
- `MAX_TIME_DELTA` can be set up to 7 days by the provider creator (permissionless factory), meaning even multi-hour outages pass the staleness check.
- The attack requires no special privilege: any user can submit a swap immediately after sequencer restart.
- The attacker can monitor the sequencer status and have a transaction ready to submit the moment it comes back online.

---

### Recommendation

Add a Chainlink Sequencer Uptime Feed check with a post-restart grace period (e.g., 1 hour) to both `PriceProviderL2` and `ProtectedPriceProviderL2`. The check should:

1. Call `sequencerUptimeFeed.latestRoundData()` and verify `answer == 0` (sequencer is up).
2. Verify `block.timestamp - startedAt >= GRACE_PERIOD` (e.g., 3600 seconds) to ensure the sequencer has been up long enough for fresh prices to have been pushed.
3. Revert with `FeedStalled()` if either condition fails.

This mirrors the fix described in the external report and the pattern already present in the `ChainlinkVerifierL2` contract in the registry.

---

### Proof of Concept

```
Setup:
- Deploy PriceProviderL2 with MAX_TIME_DELTA = 3600 (1 hour)
- Pool is live on Arbitrum with ETH/USDC pair, ETH price = $3,000

Attack:
1. L2 sequencer goes down for 30 minutes.
   - During downtime, ETH real price drops to $2,400 (20% drop).
   - No new Chainlink Data Streams reports can be pushed.

2. Sequencer restarts.
   - oracleData[feedId].price = $3,000 (pre-downtime)
   - oracleData[feedId].refTime = T_before_downtime

3. Attacker immediately calls pool.swap() (buy ETH with USDC).
   - PriceProviderL2._getBidAndAskPrice() is called.
   - _isStale(T_before_downtime, block.timestamp, 3600, FUTURE_TOLERANCE):
     - nowTs - refTime = 30 minutes = 1800 seconds < 3600 → NOT stale → passes.
   - Pool quotes ETH ask at ~$3,000.
   - Attacker buys ETH at $3,000 when real price is $2,400.

4. Attacker sells ETH on a DEX at $2,400 market price.
   - Wait — attacker BUYS ETH from the pool at the stale $3,000 ask?
   
   Correction: The attacker sells ETH INTO the pool at the stale $3,000 bid (pool buys ETH at $3,000 when it's worth $2,400), draining USDC from the pool at a 20% premium over real value.

5. LP reserves are drained by the price difference × swap size.
   - For a $1M pool, a full-depth swap at 20% stale price = ~$200,000 LP loss.
```

```text
    function _isStale(
        uint256 refTime,
        uint256 nowTs,
        uint256 maxDelta,
        uint256 futureTol
    ) internal pure returns (bool) {
        if (refTime == 0) return true;

        if (refTime > nowTs) {
            // refTime in the future: tolerate only within futureTol
            return (refTime - nowTs) > futureTol;
        }

        // refTime in the past or equal: check age
        return (nowTs - refTime) > maxDelta;
    }
```

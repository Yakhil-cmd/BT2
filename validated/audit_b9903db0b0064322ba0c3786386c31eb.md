### Title
Single Malicious Participant Can Permanently Abort DKG, Reshare, and Refresh for All Honest Parties via False Success Vote - (File: src/dkg.rs)

---

### Summary

The `broadcast_success` function in `src/dkg.rs` is called as the final step of every DKG, reshare, and refresh execution. It requires **unanimous** success votes from all participants. Because the underlying reliable echo-broadcast protocol guarantees delivery of any participant's message — including a malicious `false` vote — a single adversarial participant within the BFT tolerance can inject `(false, session_id)` at Step 5.4, causing every honest party to abort with a fatal error and permanently denying key generation for the entire group.

---

### Finding Description

`do_keyshare` is the shared core of `do_keygen`, `do_reshare`, and `do_refresh`. After all five rounds of the PedPop+ protocol complete successfully for every honest party, the function calls `broadcast_success` at line 531: [1](#0-0) 

`broadcast_success` always broadcasts `(true, session_id)` on behalf of the local participant and then collects the votes of every other participant via `do_broadcast`: [2](#0-1) 

After collection it enforces two unanimous conditions. The second one is the critical gate: [3](#0-2) 

`vote_list.iter().all(|&(boolean, _)| boolean)` — **every single vote must be `true`**. If any one entry is `false`, the function returns `ProtocolError::AssertionFailed` and the entire DKG is aborted for the calling party.

The vote collection is performed by `do_broadcast`, which wraps the reliable echo-broadcast protocol in `src/protocol/echo_broadcast.rs`: [4](#0-3) 

The echo-broadcast protocol is designed to **guarantee delivery** of whatever value a sender broadcasts, even if that sender is malicious. Its BFT tolerance is `MaxFaulty = (n-1)/3`. Within that tolerance, a malicious participant who broadcasts `(false, session_id)` will have that value reliably delivered to every honest party. There is no mechanism in `broadcast_success` to identify and skip the malicious voter; it simply checks the aggregate result and aborts on the first `false`.

Because `broadcast_success` always sends `true` for the honest caller, the only way `false` can appear in the collected `vote_list` is from a malicious participant. Yet the code treats that `false` as a fatal, unrecoverable error for all honest parties rather than identifying and excluding the offending participant.

---

### Impact Explanation

**High — Permanent denial of DKG, reshare, and refresh for honest parties.**

All three public entry points — `keygen`, `reshare`, and `refresh` — funnel through `do_keyshare`, which calls `broadcast_success` as its final step. A single malicious participant who participates honestly through rounds 1–5 (so that honest parties invest full computation and network cost) and then broadcasts `(false, session_id)` at Step 5.4 causes every honest party to abort with a fatal `ProtocolError::AssertionFailed`. The honest parties have no way to distinguish this abort from a legitimate failure, cannot identify the culprit from the error, and cannot complete the protocol without restarting with a different participant set. This permanently denies key generation, key resharing, and key refresh under valid protocol inputs and within the documented BFT trust assumptions.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

**High.** The attack requires only that the adversary control one participant — any single participant within the BFT tolerance of `floor((n-1)/3)`. The malicious action is trivial: send `(false, session_id)` instead of `(true, session_id)` in the final broadcast round. No cryptographic capability is needed. The attacker can wait until all honest parties have completed the expensive rounds 1–5 before triggering the abort, maximizing disruption. The attack is repeatable: every subsequent DKG attempt that includes the malicious participant will fail in the same way.

---

### Recommendation

Replace the unanimous-success requirement with a threshold-aware check that tolerates up to `MaxFaulty` false votes. Specifically:

1. After collecting `vote_list`, count the number of `false` votes.
2. If the count exceeds `MaxFaulty = floor((n-1)/3)`, abort and report the identified false-voters.
3. If the count is within tolerance, proceed to output the key material for the honest parties whose votes were `true`.

This mirrors the fix suggested in the external report: instead of aborting the entire operation when one sub-operation fails, isolate the failing participant and allow the remaining honest parties to complete successfully.

---

### Proof of Concept

**Setup:** 4 participants `[P0, P1, P2, P3]`, threshold = 2. BFT tolerance = `floor(3/3) = 1`, so one malicious participant is within tolerance.

**Attack steps:**

1. `P3` is malicious. All four participants call `keygen` and execute rounds 1–5 of `do_keyshare` honestly. Each honest party computes a valid signing share and verifying key.

2. At Step 5.4, `P0`, `P1`, `P2` each call `broadcast_success`, which invokes `do_broadcast` with `(true, session_id)`.

3. `P3` instead sends `(false, session_id)` through the echo-broadcast channel.

4. The reliable echo-broadcast protocol in `reliable_broadcast_receive_all` guarantees that `P3`'s `(false, session_id)` is delivered to `P0`, `P1`, and `P2` — this is the protocol's correctness guarantee.

5. In `broadcast_success` on each honest party, `vote_list` contains `(false, session_id)` for `P3`. The check at line 329: [5](#0-4) 

evaluates to `false`, and `ProtocolError::AssertionFailed("A participant seems to have failed its checks. Aborting Protocol!")` is returned.

6. `do_keyshare` propagates the error. `do_keygen` returns `Err(...)`. All three honest parties fail to obtain their key shares. The DKG is permanently aborted. No signing can ever occur with this key. [2](#0-1) [6](#0-5)
